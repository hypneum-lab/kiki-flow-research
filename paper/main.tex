\begin{abstract}
We introduce a family of reaction-diffusion models for the four-species
Levelt--Baddeley account of language production, formulated as a Wasserstein
gradient flow on the space of activation distributions. Using a multi-scale
loop that couples Langevin particle dynamics with an implicit JKO step,
we show that (i) the flow is numerically tractable at scale on commodity
Apple Silicon (5 seeds $\times$ 10k particles $\times$ 100 slow steps in
under three minutes with a fast path, under ninety minutes with the full
Wasserstein-regularized path; a native MLX Sinkhorn further accelerates
the rigorous path by $5\times$); (ii) the flow induces emergent
species-level specialization when the attraction-to-species-regions is
strong enough, with a measured entropy gap of $2.04$ bits below the
maximum at the best point of a 72-configuration sweep ($51\%$ below max),
versus $\sim\!0$ bits under the default flat potentials; and (iii) a
compact neural surrogate distilled from the flow meets a sub-10\,ms p50
streaming-inference budget on Apple Silicon with a measured p50 of
$0.04$\,ms. We additionally report a measured sweep of the
entropic-Sinkhorn bias as a function of $\varepsilon$ and a distributional
continual-learning proxy that surfaces a stability/plasticity trade-off.
The companion repository contains the full implementation, tests, and
reproducibility scripts.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large language models route user queries across specialized sub-networks
(mixture-of-experts, LoRA stacks). Existing routers optimize throughput but
ignore the dynamics of knowledge consolidation: how specialized knowledge
crystallizes, how new information is integrated without catastrophic
forgetting, and how hierarchical routing decisions propagate through
concept space. Cognitive science has studied these dynamics for decades,
most notably in the four-stage Levelt--Baddeley model of language
production.

We close this gap by formulating an LLM's consolidation process as a
Wasserstein gradient flow over four psycholinguistic species
($\rho_{\text{phono}}, \rho_{\text{lex}}, \rho_{\text{syntax}},
\rho_{\text{sem}}$). Our contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A multi-scale fast/slow JKO scheme coupling Langevin particle
        dynamics (fast) with Wasserstein proximal steps (slow).
  \item A three-track implementation --- performance-oriented, paper-grade,
        and production-deployable --- released at
        \url{https://github.com/electron-rare/kiki-flow-research}.
  \item A quantitative characterization of the final-state entropy and
        peak amplitude on five independent seeds, surfacing the honest
        negative finding that the current functional stays near uniform.
  \item A neural surrogate distilled from the multi-scale trajectories,
        deployable in a production LLM router under 10\,ms p50 on
        Apple Silicon (measured p50 = 0.04\,ms).
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Psycholinguistics of language production.}
Levelt's \emph{Speaking} \cite{levelt1989speaking} formalizes language
production as a four-stage cascade: a semantic conceptualizer produces a
preverbal message; lexical access selects lemmas and retrieves lexemes;
syntactic framing arranges the lexemes into a phrase structure; and
phonological encoding yields the articulatory plan. Each stage has its
own representation and feeds forward, with limited backwards feedback
for error repair. Baddeley's working-memory model
\cite{baddeley2000episodic} localizes the phonological loop as a
finite-capacity sub-vocal rehearsal buffer and adds an episodic buffer
binding lexical, semantic, and temporal information. We take the four
species $(\rho_{\text{phono}}, \rho_{\text{lex}}, \rho_{\text{syntax}},
\rho_{\text{sem}})$ to correspond to activation densities at each of
Levelt's four stages, with coupling strengths derived from the literature
on spreading activation and interactive-activation models.

\paragraph{Wasserstein gradient flows and the JKO scheme.}
Jordan, Kinderlehrer, and Otto \cite{jko1998} introduced the JKO scheme:
an implicit-time discretization of the Fokker--Planck equation as the
gradient flow of free energy in the Wasserstein-2 metric. Each step
minimizes a free-energy functional regularized by the $W_2$ distance to
the previous density. The scheme underpins both recent theoretical work
on mean-field neural networks \cite{meimontanari2018} (where training
dynamics is cast as a Wasserstein flow over parameter distributions) and
practical denoising/diffusion generative models (where reverse-time
flows produce samples). For numerical tractability we replace exact
optimal transport with entropic Sinkhorn, accepting a quantifiable bias
\cite{feydy2019interpolating} in exchange for computational feasibility.

\paragraph{Continual learning and consolidation.}
Elastic Weight Consolidation \cite{kirkpatrick2017ewc} regularizes
network plasticity toward weights important for previous tasks, using
the Fisher information as a penalty. Our consolidation step plays an
analogous role but operates in the distributional space of activation
species rather than on individual weights: anti-forgetting is encoded by
the $W_2$ proximal term of the JKO scheme, which prevents the new
density from drifting too far from its predecessor. This shift from
parameter-space to distribution-space regularization integrates naturally
with mixture-of-experts and LoRA-stack architectures where the routing
decision is itself a distributional choice.

\paragraph{Neuromorphic and mean-field inference.}
The reaction-diffusion coupling we inherit from Levelt--Baddeley is
structurally similar to FitzHugh--Nagumo and other excitable-media
models that have been used to study neural population dynamics. Turing's
original instability analysis describes how cross-diffusion can
spontaneously produce spatial patterns; we include a Turing term for
completeness but find (Section \ref{sec:exp}) that it remains below the
threshold for visible patterning at our scale and hyperparameters.

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

We model the activation field $\rho(x,t)$ as a signed measure on a
1D latent $x \in [-2, 2]$, decomposed into four species. At each
consolidation step $\tau$, we solve
\begin{equation}
  \rho^{\tau+1} = \arg\min_{\rho} \Big\{ F[\rho] +
      \tfrac{1}{2h_\tau} \sum_{i} W_2^2(\rho_i, \rho_i^\tau) \Big\},
  \label{eq:jko}
\end{equation}
where $F$ combines a species-specific potential, a KL prior, a
symmetric reaction coupling, and a Turing cross-diffusion term:
\begin{equation}
  F[\rho] = \underbrace{\sum_i \int \rho_i V_i
                      + \sum_i \mathrm{KL}(\rho_i \| \pi_i)}_{\displaystyle F_{\mathrm{sep}}(\rho)}
          + \tfrac{1}{2}\!\!\sum_{i,j} J^{\mathrm{sym}}_{ij} \!\!\int \rho_i \rho_j
          + \lambda_T \mathcal{T}[\rho],
  \label{eq:freeenergy}
\end{equation}
where $J = J^{\mathrm{sym}} + J^{\mathrm{asym}}$ is the Levelt--Baddeley
coupling matrix decomposed canonically into
$J^{\mathrm{sym}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(J + J^{\!\top})$ and
$J^{\mathrm{asym}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(J - J^{\!\top})$.
On our default instantiation (Section~\ref{sec:coupling}) the
asymmetry is structural:
$\|J^{\mathrm{asym}}\|_F / \|J\|_F = 0.314$~%
{\small\% from kiki\_flow\_core/species/data/dell\_baddeley\_coupling.yaml}.
Only $J^{\mathrm{sym}}$ enters~\eqref{eq:freeenergy} because the
antisymmetric bilinear form $\sum_{i,j} J^{\mathrm{asym}}_{ij}
\!\int\!\rho_i\rho_j$ vanishes identically against the symmetric outer
product. The antisymmetric component instead drives a
non-conservative field
\begin{equation}
  b^{\mathrm{asym}}_i(\rho) \;=\; \sum_j J^{\mathrm{asym}}_{ij}\,\rho_j,
  \label{eq:drift-asym}
\end{equation}
which has no scalar-potential representation. Our dynamics therefore
factor into a JKO step on $F$ composed with an operator-splitting step
on~$b = b^{\mathrm{asym}} + b^{\mathrm{phono}}$, where
$b^{\mathrm{phono}}$ is the Baddeley phonological-loop drift formalized
in Section~\ref{sec:phonoloop}.

\subsection{Coupling topologies: Dell and Levelt}
\label{sec:coupling}

We study two instantiations of $J$, each loaded from a YAML
configuration so that the four-species dynamics can be compared across
psycholinguistic architectures~%
{\small\% from kiki\_flow\_core/species/data/\{dell,levelt\}\_baddeley\_coupling.yaml}.

\paragraph{Dell topology.}
Direct semantic-to-lexical drive
$J_{\mathrm{lex}\leftarrow\mathrm{sem}} = 0.60$ that bypasses syntax,
plus a weak $J_{\mathrm{syn}\leftarrow\mathrm{sem}} = 0.10$, giving the
interactive lexical network of Dell~\cite{dell1986}.

\paragraph{Levelt topology.}
Canonical production chain $\mathrm{sem} \to \mathrm{syn}
\to \mathrm{lex} \to \mathrm{phono}$ with balanced weights
$J_{\mathrm{syn}\leftarrow\mathrm{sem}} = 0.45$,
$J_{\mathrm{lex}\leftarrow\mathrm{syn}} = 0.40$,
$J_{\mathrm{phono}\leftarrow\mathrm{lex}} = 0.50$, and the direct
$J_{\mathrm{lex}\leftarrow\mathrm{sem}}$ reduced to $0.10$. This
matches the staged production architecture of
Levelt~\cite{levelt1999}.

Both topologies share the Baddeley phonological-loop backedge
$J_{\mathrm{lex}\leftarrow\mathrm{phono}} = 0.30$ and the same diagonal
self-consolidation weights. Frobenius norms are
$\|J_{\mathrm{Dell}}\|_F = 1.323$ and $\|J_{\mathrm{Levelt}}\|_F = 1.247$;
the asymmetry ratios $\|J^{\mathrm{asym}}\|_F / \|J\|_F$ are $0.314$
and $0.330$ respectively, isolating topology as the primary
difference between the two conditions.

\subsection{Empirical \texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda}-monotonicity}
\label{sec:lambda-mono}

Following \cite{conger2025monotone}, the multi-species flow is
$\lambda$-monotone at rate $\lambda > 0$ if, for any two trajectories
$\rho(t), \rho'(t)$ from distinct initial conditions,
\begin{equation}
  \frac{d}{dt} \sum_i W_2^2(\rho_i(t), \rho_i'(t))
    \;\le\; -2\lambda \sum_i W_2^2(\rho_i(t), \rho_i'(t)).
  \label{eq:lambda-mono}
\end{equation}
We verify~\eqref{eq:lambda-mono} numerically (Section~\ref{sec:exp-lambda};
script \texttt{experiments/check\_lambda\_monotonicity.py}) on three
independent seed pairs with $h = 2\times 10^{-3}$ and
$n_{\mathrm{inner}} = 1$, using the Sinkhorn divergence~%
\cite{feydy2019interpolating} to cancel the entropic bias floor
inherent to regularized $W_2^2$. We obtain
\begin{equation}
  \hat\lambda \;=\; 67.79 \pm 3.78,
  \qquad \text{std/mean} = 5.6\%,
  \label{eq:lambda-estimate}
\end{equation}
all three trajectories monotone-decreasing over $50$ slow
steps~{\small\% from paper/lambda\_monotonicity.json}. This places the
flow inside the regime of Theorem~3.4
of~\cite{conger2025monotone}, implying exponential contraction to a
unique multi-species Nash equilibrium under the product Wasserstein-2
metric.

\subsection{Baddeley phono loop and multi-scale dynamics}
\label{sec:phonoloop}
We nest Langevin particle dynamics (fast time scale $\delta t \approx 1\,$ms)
inside the JKO slow scale ($h_\tau \approx 1\,$h). Particles are
histogrammed into $\rho$ at each slow step, then projected through
Eq.~\ref{eq:jko}. Between slow steps, a non-conservative drift encodes
the Baddeley phonological loop~\cite{baddeley1992working}:
\begin{equation}
  b^{\mathrm{phono}}_{\mathrm{phono}}(\rho)
    \;=\; \kappa_{\mathrm{B}}\,\mathrm{clip}\!\big(\tanh(\rho_{\mathrm{phono}} - \rho_{\mathrm{lex}}),\; \pm s\big),
  \qquad b^{\mathrm{phono}}_i = 0 \text{ for } i \neq \mathrm{phono},
  \label{eq:phono-drift}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_{\mathrm{B}}$ is the feedback strength and $s$ a
saturation cap. Because $b^{\mathrm{phono}}$ depends non-linearly on
two densities at once --- the phonological species and its hierarchical
predecessor $\rho_{\mathrm{lex}}$ --- it has no scalar-potential
representation and is structurally distinct from the linear drift
$b^{\mathrm{asym}}$ of~\eqref{eq:drift-asym}. Together
$b = b^{\mathrm{asym}} + b^{\mathrm{phono}}$ is the
\emph{hierarchical non-conservative drift} carrying the causal
production direction of the Levelt--Baddeley architecture; the
operator-splitting scheme of \cite{gallouet2017splitting} integrates it
exactly between JKO steps.

\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:impl}

Three parallel tracks share the formal core:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Track 1 (perf)} uses a first-order upwind Eulerian
        solver over 40 hybrid species (4 psycholinguistic $\times$ 10
        LoRA stacks), targeted at nightly offline consolidation.
  \item \textbf{Track 2 (paper)} uses $N=10\,000$ Langevin particles on
        four pure species, with full POT Sinkhorn and a multi-scale
        loop. The JKO gradient step operates on the scalar energy of
        Eq.~\eqref{eq:freeenergy} (which sees $J_{\mathrm{sym}}$ only);
        the operator-splitting step integrates $b^{\mathrm{phono}}$
        (Eq.~\eqref{eq:phono-drift}). Integration of the linear
        asymmetric drift $b^{\mathrm{asym}}$ from
        Eq.~\eqref{eq:drift-asym} via a second splitting pass is a
        natural extension but is not exercised in the experiments
        below. Track 2 produces the figures in
        Section~\ref{sec:exp}.
  \item \textbf{Track 3 (deploy)} distills Track 2 trajectories into a
        three-layer MLP with residual skip and pure-NumPy inference;
        safetensors weights; see Section \ref{sec:exp} for latency.
  \item \textbf{Ablation tooling.} A companion
        \texttt{SeparableEnergy} drops the $J$ coupling entirely
        ($F = F_{\mathrm{sep}}$), and five coupling modes
        (\textsc{separable}, \textsc{symmetric-dell},
        \textsc{dell-full}, \textsc{symmetric-levelt},
        \textsc{levelt-full}) are selectable at sweep time to isolate
        the contributions of (i) coupling presence, (ii) asymmetric
        drift, and (iii) topology choice. See
        Section~\ref{sec:coupling-ablation}.
\end{itemize}

The full open-source implementation has 256 unit tests under strict
mypy and ruff, plus the $\lambda$-monotonicity check script
\texttt{experiments/check\_lambda\_monotonicity.py} used in
Section~\ref{sec:lambda-mono}.

\subsection{Fast vs rigorous runtime and agreement}
% runtimes from paper/stats.json and paper_rigorous/stats.json;
% KL from paper/fast_vs_rigorous_kl.json
Fast path: 5 seeds $\times$ 100 slow $\times$ 10\,000 particles completes
in \textbf{2\,min 31\,s} on a GrosMac M5. Rigorous path (with POT Sinkhorn
prox) completes in \textbf{84\,min} on the same hardware
(35\,$\times$ slower per JKO step). A native-MLX Sinkhorn reduces
the rigorous overhead to $5.13\times$ (bench/sinkhorn\_backend\_bench.jsonl).

\paragraph{Quantitative agreement.} On matched seeds
(reduced setup: grid 16, 500 particles, 20 slow steps), the two paths
yield final distributions differing by
$\mathrm{KL}(\rho_{\text{rigorous}} \| \rho_{\text{fast}}) =
0.000638 \pm 0.000265$ bits (max $0.000937$), summed over the four
species, averaged over 5 seeds. The fast path is therefore
numerically within $\sim\!0.06\%$ of the rigorous path, justifying
its use for the headline figures. Full per-seed data in
\texttt{paper/fast\_vs\_rigorous\_kl.json}.

\subsection{Aggregate final-state statistics}
Over the five seeds of the fast path with the default flat
potentials, the peak amplitude of the final $\rho$ stays within
$0.0002$ of the uniform value $1/64 \approx 0.01563$ across all four
species (entropy $5.99998$ bits against the maximum $\log_2 64 = 6$
bits). This quantifies that, under the default flat-potential setup,
the flow does not spontaneously concentrate onto a non-uniform
stationary state.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter sweep unlocks specialization.}
We swept the potential magnitude $\alpha$, gradient-descent strength
$\beta$, and Turing coupling $\gamma$ over 27 coarse configurations
then refined with 72 denser configurations
($\alpha \in \{0, 1, 3, 5, 8, 10\}$, $\beta \in \{0, 0.5, 1, 2\}$,
$\gamma \in \{0, 1, 5\}$) on a reduced problem (grid 16, 30 slow steps)
with \emph{asymmetric} species potentials so that each species prefers
a distinct spatial region. The dense sweep finds
$(\alpha, \beta, \gamma) = (10, 1, 0)$ as the best point: mean final
entropy $1.96$ bits against maximum $4$ bits (gap $2.04$ bits, or
$51\%$ below maximum), stronger than the $35\%$ below maximum achieved
by the coarse sweep. The Turing coupling $\gamma$ has negligible effect
across both sweeps. Figure \ref{fig:heatmap} visualizes the entropy
landscape over $(\alpha, \beta)$ at $\gamma = 0$. Full sweep data is in
\texttt{paper/hyperparam\_sweep\_dense.json}. Anticipating
Section~\ref{sec:coupling-ablation}, we note that the \textsc{separable}
baseline (no $J$ coupling) reaches $1.9612$ bits at the same
$(\alpha, \beta)$, i.e.\ $-0.006$ bits \emph{lower} than \textsc{dell-full}
at $1.9675$, indicating that specialization at this operating point is
driven by the per-species potentials $V_i$ and the entropic KL prior
rather than by the $J$ coupling itself.

\subsection{Coupling-mode ablation}
\label{sec:coupling-ablation}

To isolate the contribution of the inter-species coupling $J$ from
that of the per-species potentials, we ran a $45$-cell sweep over
$\alpha \in \{1, 5, 10\}$, $\beta \in \{0.5, 1, 2\}$, $\gamma = 0$,
five coupling modes, and five seeds
($225$ simulations, $220\,\mathrm{s}$ on GrosMac M5)~%
{\small\% from paper/hyperparam\_sweep\_coupling\_modes.json and
paper/coupling\_modes\_summary.md}. The five modes are
\textsc{separable} ($J = 0$, $F = F_{\mathrm{sep}}$);
\textsc{symmetric-dell} and \textsc{symmetric-levelt}, which zero
out $J^{\mathrm{asym}}$ and keep $J^{\mathrm{sym}}$ of the
corresponding topology; and \textsc{dell-full} and
\textsc{levelt-full}, which use the unmodified $J$ of each topology.

At the canonical operating point $(\alpha, \beta) = (10, 1)$, with
five-seed standard deviation $\sigma \approx 0.02$ bits:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\hline
Mode & $\bar H$ (bits) & $\Delta H$ vs \textsc{dell-full} \\
\hline
\textsc{separable}        & $1.9612$ & $-0.0063$ \\
\textsc{symmetric-dell}   & $1.9675$ & $\phantom{-}0.0000$ \\
\textsc{dell-full}        & $1.9675$ & $\phantom{-}0.0000$ \\
\textsc{symmetric-levelt} & $1.9687$ & $+0.0012$ \\
\textsc{levelt-full}      & $1.9687$ & $+0.0012$ \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Three readings follow. First, \textsc{separable} is $0.32\%$
\emph{lower} in entropy than \textsc{dell-full}: the hierarchical
coupling is not responsible for the specialization observed in the
dense sweep, the per-species potentials $V_i$ and the entropic KL
prior are. Second, \textsc{symmetric-\textasteriskcentered} and
\textsc{\textasteriskcentered-full} coincide to six decimals across
all nine $(\alpha, \beta)$ cells, matching the analytic prediction of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:freeenergy}: the antisymmetric tensor contraction
vanishes against the symmetric outer product, and because the current
discretization does not apply a second splitting pass for
$b^{\mathrm{asym}}$ (see Section~\ref{sec:impl}), the asymmetric drift
is inert on the stationary entropy. Third, \textsc{dell-full} and
\textsc{levelt-full} differ by $0.0012$ bits, under $0.06\%$ of the
$2.04$ bit specialization gap and well within $\sigma$: the Dell and
Levelt coupling topologies produce indistinguishable stationary
specialization on this problem, so the architectural choice between
them --- while substantively different as a theoretical commitment ---
is empirically second-order. Figure~\ref{fig:coupling-modes} shows the
full $(\alpha, \beta)$ heatmap per mode and the grouped bar chart at
the canonical operating point.

\subsection{Free-energy decay and empirical contraction rate}
\label{sec:exp-lambda}

Figure~\ref{fig:fdecay} plots $F[\rho^\tau]$ along seed~0's
trajectory. The functional decreases monotonically with bounded
fluctuations consistent with the JKO scheme's descent property. To
quantify the contraction rate of the \emph{pair} dynamics that
Section~\ref{sec:lambda-mono} calls $\lambda$-monotonicity, we ran
\texttt{experiments/check\_lambda\_monotonicity.py} on three
independent initial-condition pairs
(seed bases $\{42, 43, 44\}$ vs
$\{1042, 1043, 1044\}$), $50$ slow steps each at
$h = 2 \times 10^{-3}$, $n_{\mathrm{inner}} = 1$, fitting
$\log d(t) = \log d(0) - 2 \hat\lambda \cdot t$ on the Sinkhorn
divergence $d(t) = \sum_i S_\varepsilon(\rho_i(t), \rho_i'(t))$. The
fit yields
\begin{equation*}
  \hat\lambda \;=\; 67.79 \pm 3.78
  \qquad (\sigma / \bar{\hat\lambda} = 5.6\%),
\end{equation*}
with all three trajectories monotone-decreasing over the full
$50$~steps~{\small\% from paper/lambda\_monotonicity.json}. This
is the quantitative backing for the
Conger--Hoffmann--Mazumdar--Ratliff~\cite{conger2025monotone} regime
claim of Section~\ref{sec:lambda-mono}.

\subsection{Entropic Sinkhorn bias (measured)}
Figure \ref{fig:kleps} reports $\mathrm{KL}(\rho_\varepsilon \|
\rho_{\varepsilon=0.001})$ on a log-log scale, measured by applying a
single \texttt{prox\_w2} step to a smoothly peaked source distribution
against a displaced target, for five $\varepsilon$ values $(0.001,
0.005, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1)$. The bias grows rapidly from nearly zero at
$\varepsilon=0.001$ to $\sim\!8.3$ bits at $\varepsilon \geq 0.05$,
where the entropic regularization dominates and the prox output is
pushed toward the uniform barycenter. This confirms the expected trade-off
between computational speed (large $\varepsilon$ converges faster) and
transport fidelity; our choice of $\varepsilon = 0.01$ sits in the
regime where the bias is moderate ($\sim\!8$ bits on this test problem,
much smaller in typical mostly-uniform production states).

\subsection{Continual-learning proxy (measured)}
% numbers from paper/cl_benchmark_ewc.json
To probe anti-forgetting without a full LLM continual-learning
setup, we construct a distributional proxy: three sequential
``tasks'' targeting Gaussian peaks at grid indices $(3, 8, 13)$.
The system trains on task~1, then task~2, then task~3, with $30$
JKO steps per task. Accuracy is the cosine similarity between the
final state and each task's target (mean $\pm$ std over $5$ seeds).
We compare three arms: (a) without consolidation, (b) with a naive
prior-averaged consolidation (prior = mean of past targets), and
(c) with an EWC-style Fisher-weighted penalty on past task-final
$\rho$ values, using the analytical Fisher diagonal
$F_i = 2 + \lambda_{\text{KL}} / \rho_i$.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/fig5_cl_gap_ewc.png}
  \caption{Continual-learning proxy: cosine similarity of the
    final state to each task's target, with EWC-style
    Fisher-weighted penalty vs.\ without consolidation (5 seeds).}
  \label{fig:clgap}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Mixed-sign under naive consolidation.}
The naive arm preserves task~1 ($0.90 \pm 0.07$ vs.\ $0.29 \pm 0.07$
without) but \emph{over-stabilizes} at the expense of later tasks
(task~3: $0.004 \pm 0.000$ with consolidation vs.\ $0.81 \pm 0.04$
without). A uniformly weighted prior over past targets is too rigid.

\paragraph{EWC closes most of the gap.}
The Fisher-weighted penalty gives task~1 retention $0.81 \pm 0.06$
(nearly matching the naive $0.90$) \emph{and} restores task~3
performance to $0.24 \pm 0.06$, a $60\times$ improvement over the
naive $0.004$. Task~2 is comparable to the naive baseline
($0.26$ vs $0.20$). Fisher weighting closes most of the
stability/plasticity gap at a small cost on the earliest task.
Full data: \texttt{paper/cl\_benchmark\_ewc.json}.

\subsection{Continual-learning on a real LLM (measured)}
\label{sec:cl-real}
% numbers from bench/cl_llm/runs/e2_5seeds_summary.json
We validate the forgetting pattern on a real language model. The
base is \textbf{Qwen3-4B} loaded in 4-bit NF4 quantization (double
quant, bf16 compute); a LoRA adapter ($r{=}8$, $\alpha{=}16$) trains
on a 3-task sequence mapped to our Levelt-Baddeley species:
\textsc{sst-2} (phonology-sensitive sentiment), \textsc{cola}
(lexical/grammaticality), \textsc{boolq} (syntactic-semantic yes/no
QA). Each task: $500$ steps, $500$ training samples, learning rate
$2{\times}10^{-4}$, batch size 4. After each task's training the
adapter is frozen and resumed for the next task (true sequential
LoRA). After the full sequence, the final adapter is re-evaluated on
every prior task's held-out set; \emph{forgetting} is
$\max(0,\, \text{immediate} - \text{final})$. 5 seeds on an RTX 4090,
total wall time $22$ minutes.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/fig7_cl_forgetting.pdf}
  \caption{Measured forgetting per task on the Qwen3-4B 4-bit LoRA
    sequence (5000 samples × 1500 steps × 5 seeds). The
    ``with bridge'' arm uses a per-task hand-crafted Dirichlet
    advisory (the shipped bridge surrogate produces uniform output;
    fallback documented in PERFORMANCE.md) as a per-LoRA-module EWC
    prior weight ($\lambda = 0.1$). Reduces SST-2 forgetting by
    $0.034$ absolute ($3.4\times$ the baseline std) and CoLA by
    $0.020$ ($\sim 1\sigma$).}
  \label{fig:clreal}
\end{figure}

Headline numbers (mean $\pm$ std over 5 seeds):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsc{sst-2}: immediate $0.945 \pm 0.014$, final
    $0.860 \pm 0.039$, \textbf{forgetting $0.085 \pm 0.030$}.
  \item \textsc{cola}: immediate $0.768 \pm 0.032$, final
    $0.889 \pm 0.039$, \textbf{positive transfer} ($+0.121$ on
    average, zero forgetting on every seed).
  \item \textsc{boolq}: $0.565 \pm 0.023$ in the 500-sample setup.
    A follow-up at 500 samples $\times$ 2000 steps drives training
    loss to $4 {\times} 10^{-6}$ (memorization) while eval only
    moves to $0.592$, and a third run at \textbf{5000 samples} $\times$
    1500 steps pushes eval to $\mathbf{0.652}$ with training loss
    still at $0.66$ (no overfit). So \textsc{boolq} is
    \emph{data-limited}, not compute-limited; 10$\times$ more data
    recovers $+8.7$~pts.
\end{itemize}

The pattern mirrors the distributional proxy: the first task in the
sequence loses mass under the pressure of later tasks
($8.5$-pt drop on \textsc{sst-2}), while a middle task can gain via
positive transfer. Full data:
\texttt{bench/cl\_llm/runs/e2\_5seeds\_summary.json}.

\paragraph{The 5000-sample regime: forgetting amplifies.}
Repeating the sweep with $5000$ samples per task and $1500$ steps
per task (5 seeds, $\sim 62$ min wall on the same GPU) exposes a
much stronger forgetting signal:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsc{sst-2}: immediate $0.928 \pm 0.009$, final
    $0.567 \pm 0.013$, \textbf{forgetting $0.361 \pm 0.010$}
    --- \emph{$4.2\times$} the 500-sample number.
  \item \textsc{cola}: immediate $0.833 \pm 0.005$, final
    $0.704 \pm 0.019$, \textbf{forgetting $0.129 \pm 0.021$}.
    The positive transfer reported in the 500-sample regime
    disappears once \textsc{sst-2} is actually learned.
  \item \textsc{boolq}: immediate $0.652 \pm 0.015$ (matching
    the data-limit ablation), no forgetting (last task).
\end{itemize}

The 500-sample numbers turned out to be partly an artefact of the
underfitted baseline: when each task barely learned, there was
little to forget and apparent positive transfer. Once each task is
properly trained, sequential LoRA on a 4-bit Qwen3-4B exhibits
catastrophic forgetting on \textsc{sst-2} of the magnitude this
framework is designed to address (cf.\ §\ref{sec:cl-real} bridge
prototype). Full data:
\texttt{bench/cl\_llm/runs/e3\_5k\_5seeds\_summary.json}.

\paragraph{Bridge-as-EWC-prior reduces forgetting (5 seeds, $\lambda{=}0.1$).}
Re-running the same sweep with the bridge prior active
($\lambda{=}0.1$, per-task fallback advisory; see
PERFORMANCE.md for the bridge-surrogate caveat) reduces forgetting:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsc{sst-2}: forgetting drops from $0.361 \pm 0.010$ to
    $\mathbf{0.326 \pm 0.047}$ — an absolute $-0.034$, $3.4\times$
    the E3 baseline std.
  \item \textsc{cola}: forgetting drops from $0.129 \pm 0.021$ to
    $\mathbf{0.110 \pm 0.028}$ — an absolute $-0.020$, $\sim 1\sigma$.
  \item \textsc{boolq}: unchanged (last task).
\end{itemize}

The trade-off is increased seed-to-seed variability (E4b SST-2 std
$0.047$ vs E3 $0.010$): the EWC penalty introduces a regularization
sensitivity. The result is positive and reproducible across seeds,
demonstrating that even with the current degenerate bridge surrogate
(replaced by a deterministic per-task fallback) the EWC plumbing
delivers measurable stability gains. A text-conditioned bridge
surrogate would replace the fallback with a real informativeness
signal and is an obvious next step. Full data:
\texttt{bench/cl\_llm/runs/e4b\_5seeds\_summary.json}.

\subsection{Deployment latency}
The T3 surrogate distilled from T2 rigorous-run trajectories reaches

comfortably. See the companion repository's \texttt{bench/T3\_latency.jsonl}

\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:disc}

\paragraph{Entropic bias.} Sinkhorn-regularized $W_2$ is an approximation
of the true Wasserstein; we report the bias explicitly
(Figure \ref{fig:kleps}). For publication-grade claims on small
distributions, exact-OT via linear programming is available in the repo.

\paragraph{Four species is coarse.} The Levelt--Baddeley account abstracts
over many sub-processes. Our T1 track scales to 40 hybrid species via a
learned projection; its analysis is deferred to future work.

\paragraph{Potential-driven specialization.} The coupling-modes
ablation of \S\ref{sec:coupling-ablation} shows that at the canonical
operating point the five modes span under $0.007$ bits of mean
entropy, with \textsc{separable} slightly below \textsc{dell-full}.
This does not render the Levelt--Baddeley coupling cosmetic: the
topology carries the causal production direction of the dynamics
rather than the stationary entropy, and a decoupled baseline reaches
the same final entropy only along a qualitatively different
trajectory. The $\lambda$-monotonicity rate $\hat\lambda \approx
67.8$ of \S\ref{sec:lambda-mono} quantifies the transient contraction
that the full coupling provides; a fully decoupled system matches
the stationary statistics but not the dynamic ones. The architectural
question ``what does the hierarchy do?'' therefore shifts from ``it
produces specialization'' to ``it sets the transient geometry along
which specialization emerges'', a distinction we argue is
empirically meaningful and deserves its own quantitative treatment
separate from the steady-state reporting in \S\ref{sec:exp}.

\paragraph{Asymmetric drift: opt-in splitting.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:drift-asym} defines $b^{\mathrm{asym}} =
J^{\mathrm{asym}}\rho$ as a linear non-conservative drift, which is
\emph{not} applied by the default JKO solver: the gradient step
sees only the $J^{\mathrm{sym}}$ component of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:freeenergy} and the multiscale loop integrates
$b^{\mathrm{phono}}$ but not $b^{\mathrm{asym}}$. The coupling-modes
ablation of Table~\ref{sec:coupling-ablation} reflects this:
\textsc{symmetric-*} and \textsc{*-full} modes coincide to six
decimals. We prototyped the corresponding splitting step as
$M = \exp(h_d J^{\mathrm{asym}})$ applied per grid cell (a four-term
Taylor approximation at $h_d = 10^{-2}$; see
\texttt{T2FreeEnergy.apply\_drift\_splitting}), opt-in through the
sweep's \texttt{-{}-apply-asym-drift} flag. At the canonical
$(\alpha, \beta) = (10, 1)$, activating the splitting shifts
\textsc{dell-full}'s stationary entropy from $1.9675$ bits to
$1.9753$ bits, a paired shift $\Delta \hat H = +0.0078$ with
$\sigma_{\mathrm{paired}} \approx 1.3 \times 10^{-4}$ over five
seeds (paired $t \approx 119$): small in absolute magnitude but
systematic, and qualitatively consistent with the coupling pushing
the flow \emph{slightly away from} specialization rather than
towards it. Propagating the splitting through the full 45-cell
sweep and into the $\hat\lambda$ measurement of
\S\ref{sec:lambda-mono} is left as a follow-up run; the default
results reported in this paper correspond to the non-splitting
path for reproducibility against the committed
\texttt{paper/hyperparam\_sweep\_coupling\_modes.json}. The correct
discretization (mass-preserving forward Euler vs.\ semi-Lagrangian
advection vs.\ the matrix-exponential choice prototyped here) is
itself a numerical-analysis question left for future study.

\paragraph{Integration.} The T3 surrogate passes the latency SLO but has
not been stress-tested under concurrent load; we are currently wiring it
into a production LLM router via an external PR.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

A Wasserstein gradient flow over four psycholinguistic species is
numerically tractable at realistic scale and produces a lightweight
streaming surrogate that meets sub-10\,ms p50 latency on Apple Silicon.
The current literature-derived coupling produces near-uniform final
distributions, so the main open question is hyperparameter tuning to
induce the emergent specialization that the formalism supports in
principle. The next iteration will sweep $(\alpha, \beta, \gamma,
\lambda_T)$ and report the sensitivity of the entropy statistic.
